The Bhaktamar Stotra: A Timeless Monument of Jain Devotion

The Bhaktamar Stotra is a profound Sanskrit prayer in Jainism, composed by 7th-century monk Acharya Manatunga. Dedicated to Bhagwan Rishabhanatha, it glorifies the virtues of all twenty-four Tirthankaras. It exemplifies pure devotion (*Bhakti*), transcending all spiritual obstacles.

I. Compositional and Philosophical Foundations

The Author and Era

Acharya Manatunga chose the Vasantatilaka meter for the hymn, contributing to its rhythmic energy. The sound (*Mantra*) itself acts as a spiritual catalyst.

Name Origin

  • Bhakta: The devotee.
  • Amar: Immortal, representing liberation (*Moksha*).

Hence, Bhaktamar signifies devotion leading to an immortal state of Siddha.

Structure and Sectarian Variation

The Stotra is structured with progressive verses. Total verses differ between sects:

  • Digambara: 48 verses (*Shlokas*).
  • Śvetāmbara: 44 verses (plus 4 often added later).

II. The Legend of the 48 Chains (*Katha*)

Acharya Manatunga was confined with 48 chains. Focusing on Bhagwan Rishabhanatha, he composed the Stotra, and each verse shattered a chain, symbolizing liberation from karmic bonds.

III. Practice: The Trinity of Power (Shloka, Yantra, Mantra)

A. The Shloka (Verse)
Verses act as affirmations of the Tirthankara’s perfection, neutralizing negative karma.
B. The Yantra (Mystical Diagram)
Each verse has a unique Yantra, a geometric diagram for meditation and focus.
C. The Mantra
Embedded mantras provide concentrated sonic energy, recited to activate the verse’s benefit.

IV. Comprehensive Benefits and Application

Chanting the Stotra with faith yields protection, healing, prosperity, and inner growth.
